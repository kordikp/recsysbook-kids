Every recommendation system serves three groups at the same time. And those groups want very different things.

**You** want content that's fun, interesting, useful, or all three. You want to discover new things, laugh, learn, and feel good about how you spent your time.

**The platform** (YouTube, TikTok, Instagram) wants you to stay on the app as long as possible. More time on the app means more ads shown. More ads means more money. That's how free apps make billions of dollars.

**Creators** want views, followers, and engagement. Their livelihood -- or at least their popularity -- depends on the algorithm showing their content to people.

When these three goals line up, everybody wins. A creator makes something genuinely great. The algorithm shows it to you. You love it. You stay on the app because you're having a good time. The platform makes money. Everybody's happy.

But what happens when the goals *conflict*?

Think about clickbait. A creator makes a video with a wild, misleading thumbnail and title: "YOU WON'T BELIEVE WHAT HAPPENED!" You click. The video is disappointing. But you still clicked, and you still watched for 30 seconds before you realized it was junk.

From the algorithm's perspective, that was a *success*. You clicked. You watched. Mission accomplished.

From YOUR perspective, it was a waste of time.

This isn't a small problem. YouTube's own researchers published a paper in 2019 admitting that their recommendation system had been suggesting increasingly extreme and shocking content -- not because anyone wanted that, but because extreme content gets more clicks. The algorithm did exactly what it was told to do. It just wasn't told to do the right thing.

**Think about it:** The algorithm isn't evil. It doesn't have opinions or intentions. It's a math equation doing what it was programmed to do. The real question is bigger than any algorithm: *who gets to decide what it's told to do?* The company? The government? The users? You?
